# skill-researcher-litreview

A profession-shaped NeuroDock skill for the three things someone doing a literature survey actually does: scaffold a review into atomic reading and synthesis tasks, record the papers they encounter so the graph remembers them, and ask later what a given author has written. Three trigger phrases, three flows, no overlap with the general-purpose planning or recall skills.

The skill supports the act of reading and synthesising deeply. It is not a productivity hack and does not help anyone read faster.

## Who this is for

- **Academic researchers** running a literature survey at the start of a project, paper, or grant proposal.
- **PhD students** building the lit-review chapter and trying to keep track of fifty-plus papers across multiple sub-themes.
- **Lit-heavy industry researchers** — UX researchers, ML researchers, policy analysts, clinicians, librarians — who do real synthesis work, not just "I read the abstract on the train".
- **Anyone doing a literature survey for the first time** and looking for a structured way in.

It is **not** for:

- **Citation management.** Use [Zotero](https://www.zotero.org/), Mendeley, or Paperpile. This skill does not export BibTeX, store PDFs, deduplicate against a remote catalogue, or integrate with Word/LaTeX. The graph remembers paper + author + authorship as plain facts so the skill can recall what _you_ recorded — that is its only scope.
- **Paper search.** It does not query Google Scholar, Semantic Scholar, arXiv, OpenAlex, PubMed, or any other index. Finding papers is the user's job; this skill helps them keep track of what they found.
- **Summarisation.** It does not generate abstracts, "key takeaways", or "what this paper is about". Read the paper.
- **Productivity coaching.** It does not help anyone read more papers per hour. If anything, it slows the user down by asking them to record each paper explicitly.

## What it does

Three activation paths. The trigger phrase determines the flow.

| Trigger                                   | Flow                     | What it produces                                                                                                                                       |
| ----------------------------------------- | ------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| `start a lit review on <topic>`           | Lit-review decomposition | A list of atomic reading and synthesis tasks (5–90 minutes each, with acceptance criteria) generated by `neurodock-task-fractionator`. No seed papers. |
| `add a paper: <title>, <authors>, <year>` | Record paper             | Graph facts persisted via `neurodock-cognitive-graph`: one paper entity (tagged `paper`), one year tag, one `belongs_to` edge per author.              |
| `recall papers by <author>`               | Recall by author         | A plaintext bibliography of every paper recorded for that author. Never invents a paper.                                                               |

See `SKILL.md` for the full operating instructions and triggers.

## Framing — understanding, not throughput

The whole reason this skill exists separately from a general planner is that literature review is not a throughput problem. Reading a paper carefully takes the time it takes. Synthesising fifteen papers into a coherent argument takes the time it takes. Tools that try to compress this work usually substitute summary for understanding — and summary is not what a literature review is for.

What this skill helps with:

- Breaking a vague topic into concrete, finite tasks so the work has a shape.
- Remembering which papers you have read, by whom, and when — so you can come back in three months and ask the graph instead of your memory.
- Letting the graph answer "what has <author> written" honestly — only what you recorded, with no fabricated papers.

What this skill deliberately does NOT do:

- It does not summarise papers.
- It does not propose seed papers.
- It does not rate or rank papers ("highly cited", "seminal", "influential" — the skill has no basis for any such claim).
- It does not editorialise about a topic ("rich literature", "active area").

## What it explicitly does NOT do

- It does not invent papers, authors, years, venues, DOIs, or abstracts. Ever.
- It does not summarise papers, abstracts, or "key contributions".
- It does not propose seed papers in the decomposition flow. The user identifies their own seed papers — that is the first task.
- It does not export to BibTeX, RIS, EndNote, or any other citation format. Use Zotero.
- It does not store PDFs or full-text. Use Zotero.
- It does not query any external service. No network calls.
- It does not silently merge author names. `Bender E.` and `Emily M. Bender` are two entities until the user explicitly aliases them.
- It does not use productivity vocabulary: no "consume", "crush", "power through", "10x your literature review", "optimise your reading".
- It does not rate, rank, or score papers or authors.

## Why these three flows together

These are the three things any sustained literature survey requires: a structure for the work, a way to record what you've found, and a way to ask later what you found. They share a common shape (messy human input → structured artifact → stop) and they reinforce each other across sessions: the decomposition in Flow A names "read first batch" as a task; Flow B is how you record what you read in that batch; Flow C is how you come back two weeks later and recover the state. The skill is profession-shaped — splitting these into three plugins would mean someone running a lit review has to install three things.

## Dependencies

| MCP server                    | Required? | Used for                                                                                                                                    |
| ----------------------------- | --------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| `neurodock-task-fractionator` | **Hard**  | The `decompose` tool in Flow A. Without it, lit-review decomposition cannot run.                                                            |
| `neurodock-cognitive-graph`   | **Hard**  | `record_fact` in Flow B (paper + authors + authorship); `recall_entity` in Flow C (papers by author). Without it, Flows B and C cannot run. |

Both are hard requirements declared in `plugin.yaml` under `requires.mcp_servers`. There is no useful degraded mode: decomposition is the load-bearing piece of Flow A, and graph recall/record is the load-bearing piece of Flows B and C. The skill refuses to activate when either server is unavailable.

## How to install

Use the NeuroDock CLI (requires `@neurodock/cli` ≥ 0.4.0). Run from the repo root:

```sh
# Install
neurodock plugin add ./plugins/skill-researcher-litreview

# Activate
neurodock plugin enable skill-researcher-litreview

# Restart your MCP client (Claude Desktop, Claude Code, Cursor)

# Verify
neurodock plugin list
```

The plugin should appear with `trust: community` and the three triggers listed above. `plugin validate ./plugins/skill-researcher-litreview` will check the manifest before install if you want to dry-run.

## How to use

Once installed, just say one of the trigger phrases in your normal LLM client. The skill is profile-agnostic (`neurotypes: []`) so it auto-activates for any user whose profile allows community plugins.

Examples:

> Start a lit review on embodied cognition in HCI.

> Add a paper: Where Action Meets Cognition, Anderson and Lee, 2019.

> Recall papers by Bender.

The skill replies with a task list, a record confirmation, or a bibliography (respectively). It does not propose follow-up questions, ask "anything else?", or otherwise extend the conversation. When the artifact is ready, the skill stops.

## When NOT to use it

- **You need a BibTeX export for your LaTeX document.** Use Zotero — it does this well and this skill does not do it at all.
- **You want a summary of a paper you haven't read.** This skill will not summarise. Read the paper, or use a different tool, knowing what you give up.
- **You want recommendations for related work.** The skill does not recommend. It only recalls what you recorded.
- **You're trying to read more papers in less time.** Wrong tool. Wrong framing.

## Decisions flagged for human review

Two design choices in this skill are deliberate but worth a maintainer's second look before the plugin moves out of `0.1.0`:

1. **Bibliography format is plaintext `<author>. (<year>). <title>.`** — not BibTeX, not strict APA, not Chicago. The reasoning: the skill is explicitly not a citation manager, and emitting BibTeX would invite users to treat it as one. APA and Chicago both require fields the skill never records (venue, page numbers, DOI). Plaintext keeps the boundary clear: this is a recall, not a citation. **Maintainer review**: confirm this matches the project's stance, or propose a switch to BibTeX with a separate "citation export" flag.

2. **Papers are recorded as `concept` entities, not as a dedicated `paper` type.** — The v0.1.0 cognitive-graph entity-type vocabulary does not include `paper`. Using `concept` plus a `tagged: paper` fact is the closest fit. **Maintainer review**: if the v0.2 cognitive-graph type-extensions mechanism lands, this should be revisited to use a proper `paper` type. The migration is straightforward (rewrite the entity type; the `tagged: paper` fact becomes redundant).

3. **`belongs_to` is used for the authorship edge.** — The v0.1.0 eight-predicate vocabulary (`mentioned_in`, `decided_in`, `reports_to`, `depends_on`, `resolved_by`, `blocked_by`, `tagged`, `belongs_to`) does not include `authored_by` or `written_by`. `belongs_to` is the closest fit and reads as "the paper belongs to its authors". **Maintainer review**: when type-extensions land, swap to a proper `authored_by` predicate.

## Pairing with other skills

- Pair with `adhd-daily-planner` (in-tree) if you want a morning brief that surfaces "you have lit-review reading scheduled today" so you can chain into the work.
- Pair with `audhd-context-recovery` (in-tree) when returning to a literature review after a break — `where was I` first to reconstruct the lit-review state, then `recall papers by <author>` to remember what you had on file for each key author.
